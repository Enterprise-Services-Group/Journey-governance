Build it well, then hand over the keys

You build the journey, upload the insights and tell the story. You are not its permanent custodian. Your job is to leave behind a journey that is trusted, evidenced and owned.

Purpose

Before a single stage is mapped, confirm the journey deserves to exist: it has an owner, a clear purpose, and the right scope. This is where most governance debt is created or avoided.

Confirm Ownership

  • Who owns this journey?
  • Who will maintain it after the project?
  • Has a Journey Owner been identified?
Rule: No journey should be created without a nominated owner.

Confirm Purpose

  • Why is this journey being created?
  • What decision will it support?
  • What problem is it trying to solve?
Rule: Journeys must have a clear purpose.

Confirm Scope

  • What level is this, L0, L1, L2?
  • Enterprise, service, team or project level?
  • Does a journey like this already exist?
Rule: Avoid duplicates. Search existing journeys first.

Common risks

  • Building "because we always map" with no decision to support
  • No owner lined up, so the journey is orphaned at day one
  • A near-identical journey already exists in another faculty
  • Scope creep, an L1 quietly becoming three L2s

Success indicators

  • A named Journey Owner has agreed before mapping starts
  • The supporting decision and audience are written down
  • An Ask TheyDo audit confirmed no duplicate exists
  • Level and scope are explicit and signed off
Purpose

Journey creation rarely starts with fully validated insights. It starts with informed assumptions and emerging research. Your job in this stage is to build honestly from what you know, and to be clear about how confident you are in each insight.

From Assumptions to Validated Insights

A journey is usually built from a mix of known pain points, emerging research, and informed assumptions. That is normal and expected. What matters is that insights are not treated as proven simply because they exist in TheyDo. Validation happens progressively as the project matures.

  1. Understand the problem first. Start from the known pain points and what the team already understands about the experience.
  2. Conduct your research. Interviews, surveys, analytics, service data, support requests, workshop outputs.
  3. Do light manual synthesis, a 60 / 40 approach. Complete around 40% of the synthesis manually. This is where you build the tacit knowledge that lets you judge the data well, you get to know it intimately.
  4. Upload the research output into TheyDo. Let TheyDo help generate the initial journey and insights. Because your 40% manual work is done, you can accept or reject the AI-generated insights accurately, you know the data.
  5. Set an honest status. At this point most insights are an Assumption or Needs Validation. Do not mark them Validated just because they have been uploaded.
  6. Validate progressively. As the project matures, evidence builds. Insights move through Needs Validation, In Discussion and Priority Insight, and near the end you move them to Validated where appropriate.
!
Governance guidance: Insights should move through different levels of confidence throughout the lifecycle of a project. Uploading an insight into TheyDo does not automatically make it validated. Validation occurs progressively as evidence is gathered and findings are reviewed.

Purpose · Governance principle

Journeys are living assets. They require maintenance. A journey that is not maintained quietly becomes misleading, and a misleading journey is worse than none.

Who actually does the maintenance?

The Journey Owner is accountable, but is not always the person doing the day-to-day work. The Owner may be the senior sponsor or accountable business owner, and may delegate hands-on maintenance to a Sub-Journey Owner.

Journey Owner

Accountable, senior sponsor

  • Holds standing accountability for the journey
  • Approves changes and signs off direction
  • May delegate day-to-day maintenance
  • Champions the journey inside the business
Sub-Journey Owner

Delegated day-to-day maintainer

  • Updates insights and adds new evidence
  • Reviews journey accuracy
  • Flags outdated content
  • Keeps the journey usable
  • Escalates issues to the Journey Owner or Journey Manager

The Sub-Journey Owner does NOT need to know TheyDo back to front

They only need to understand enough to do the job well.

They only need to know enough to
  • Add or update insights
  • Maintain journey content
  • Review journey health
  • Avoid duplication
  • Know when to ask for help

Documenting your insights in TheyDo

TheyDo is the primary insights repository, every project should be documented in it. The most valuable journey maps grow alongside the work. This guide brings together the design team's shared knowledge: not just what to do, but why it matters.

What we use TheyDo for, and when

TheyDo is a cloud based journey management platform: a central repository that overlays insights across journeys, turning isolated research into a searchable, reusable organisational asset any service team can build on.

With TheyDo

  • Single source of truth across the University
  • Insights linked to journeys, opportunities and evidence
  • Build on what the team already knows
  • Monitor the impact of change over time

Without TheyDo

  • Insights stuck in PowerPoint, Miro and SharePoint
  • Disconnected and hard to find
  • Rediscovered from scratch by the next team
  • Decisions based on assumption, not learning
✓
When to use it: throughout the life of a project. The earlier you start, the more value it provides. Set up phases, steps, insight data fields and metrics, even as placeholders, from day one.

The journey framework, L0 to L3

TheyDo uses a four-level hierarchy. Knowing your level keeps journeys in the right place and avoids duplication.

L0

Lifecycle Framework

The highest-level phases of the constituent experience, holistic and end to end.

L1

Service Experience

How a specific service unfolds stage by stage for most users, frontstage and backstage.

L2

Experience Variations

Divergent or parallel steps, edge cases, key segments or critically different scenarios.

L3

Micro Journey

A detailed product or platform-level journey, or a recurring service-agnostic journey.

✓
If your project is student based, you most likely won't need a new L0, use the existing SSTP, Service Experience Improvement workspace.

Setting up your journey map

A solid foundation early means everything you add later, insights, opportunities, solutions, research, has a clear place to live.

Check for an existing journey, three ways

  • Browse the Journey Frameworks section in the left panel
  • Use the search function under Journeys
  • Ask TheyDo: "Does a journey already exist for [project / topic]?"
If one exists, contribute to it rather than creating a new one.

Choose how to start, three options

  • From a saved template, always check first. The L1 Service Experience template is available; use it as a starting point even for L2/L3 if nothing fits.
  • From scratch, build manually, using an existing journey as reference.
  • From Data Hub sources, upload research (TXT/CSV, up to 30 files), assign a source type, and let Journey AI generate a starting structure to review and edit.
Remember: templates give you a starting point, not a constraint. Add, remove or adjust lanes and phases afterwards.

Name it and write a description

Give it a clear name and a description covering: what the journey is about, who it involves, and what its scope covers.

Often skipped, first thing read. A clear description helps new team members, and future you, understand the purpose without digging through content.

Define phases, steps and lanes

  • Phases: click the + between phases, name it short and descriptive, drag to reposition.
  • Steps: hover between steps, click +, name it (placeholders are fine), drag into position.
  • Lanes: hover between lanes, click + Add lane, choose the type.

Two lane families:

  • Building block content (Insights, Opportunities, Solutions, Metrics, Nested Journeys), reusable and relational. Always include Insights, Opportunities and Solutions, they form the chain of evidence.
  • Basic content (Channels, Text, Image, Links, Tags), static, add based on project needs.

Naming conventions

  • Use a clear, descriptive name a colleague would recognise without context
  • Reflect the level in the name where helpful (for example, the service for an L1)
  • Avoid working titles, project codenames or personal shorthand
  • Keep naming consistent with sibling journeys in the same workspace
Why it matters: consistent names make journeys findable and prevent accidental duplicates.

Scope and ownership before you build

  • Decide where the journey begins and ends before adding phases
  • Confirm the level, L0, L1, L2 or L3, and that it fits the hierarchy
  • Identify the nominated Journey Owner, no journey should exist without one
  • Confirm the correct workspace, journeys are hard to move later
Rule: Scope, level and owner are agreed before a single phase is mapped.

Documenting your insights

Insights are qualitative data about your constituents, broken into bite-sized pieces mapped across the journey. They come from interviews, surveys, support logs, research reports or feedback.

Factual insight

Observed directly, free of assumptions

"Students report they do not know where to submit a complaint."

Interpreted insight

A conclusion drawn, a need, pain or gain

"Students need a single, clearly signposted channel for submitting complaints."

i
Both are valid. What matters is being consistent in how you classify them, and honest about what is evidence based versus assumed.

Insight types

TypeWhat it captures
PainA friction point, frustration or barrier that negatively affects the experience.
GainA positive experience or strength worth preserving or amplifying.
NeedAn unmet requirement, functional or emotional, expressed or inferred.
BehaviourAn observable behaviour, workaround or pattern in how users act.
ExpectationWhat users believe should happen, regardless of whether it does.
Key MomentA moment of heightened impact, transition or decision in the journey.
ObservationA factual note about behaviour or context, not yet interpreted.
QuoteA verbatim statement from a constituent illustrating an experience or finding.
✓
Pain or Observation? Ask: does this negatively affect the experience? If yes, it's a Pain. If it just happens or is observed, it's an Observation.

Before an insight earns a place in the journey, it should pass a simple quality test:

A good insight is

  • Specific, not a vague generalisation

  • Honestly classified, factual or interpreted
  • Not a duplicate of an existing insight

Validation status guidance

Insights move through different levels of confidence across a project's lifecycle. An insight is not validated simply because it exists in TheyDo, validation occurs progressively as evidence is gathered and findings are reviewed.

Assumption→ Needs Validation→ In Discussion→ Priority Insight→ Validated

Two end states sit outside the main flow: Archived and Cancelled.

Assumption
An observation or hypothesis that has not yet been supported by evidence.
When to use: at the very start, capturing what you think you know. Example: "Students probably abandon enrolment because the form is too long."
Needs Validation
Evidence exists but further validation is required before confidence can be established.
When to use: one data point or a single source suggests something. Example: "Two interviews mention confusion at payment, worth confirming with analytics."
In Discussion
The insight is currently being reviewed, discussed or challenged by stakeholders or the project team.
When to use: the insight is contested or its meaning is being worked through. Example: "Team disagrees on whether this is a UX issue or a policy issue."
Priority Insight
A significant insight identified as important to decision-making, but not yet formally validated.
When to use: flag something that matters and needs attention now. Example: "Drop-off at ID verification could affect the whole cohort, prioritise validating this."
Validated
Supported through research, evidence, stakeholder review and project validation activities.
When to use: multiple sources or participants confirm it and it reflects the current experience. Example: "Confirmed by interviews, analytics and support data, students cannot find where to submit a complaint."
Archived
No longer relevant, but retained for historical reference.
When to use: the insight related to a process that has since changed. Example: "Refers to the old enrolment system replaced in 2025, kept for context."
Cancelled
Reviewed and determined to be inaccurate, no longer relevant, duplicated or unsuitable for continued use.
When to use: the insight was wrong or duplicates another. Example: "Duplicate of an existing validated insight, cancelled to avoid confusion."

Linking your research sources

Linking insights to their source is what makes your journey map trustworthy and reusable. Without it, anyone picking up the work later has no way of knowing where an insight came from. Do it early, tracing sources after the fact is one of the most time-consuming parts of working in TheyDo.

How to add a source
  1. Open the insight card by clicking on it.
  2. Click Add property and select Source.
  3. Select the source tag that best describes where it came from. You can select multiple tags and attach or link multiple files per insight.

Journey health indicators

A healthy journey shows these signs. If any are missing, it is due for attention:

Signs of a healthy journey

  • Has a named owner
  • Insights are less than 12 months old
  • Reviewed within the last quarter
  • Insights trace back to evidence
  • No duplicate versions exist

Signs it needs attention

  • No clear owner, or the owner has moved on
  • Stale insights with no recent review
  • Insights with no source or unclear status
  • Duplicates or fragmented versions appearing
  • The service has changed but the journey has not

The primary governance reference

The Eight Principles in Practice

These principles guide how we create, maintain and evolve journeys to ensure they remain trusted, connected, actionable and valuable over time. This is the working governance reference for Journey Creators, Owners and Managers.

Each principle expands with a practical example, a sign of what good governance looks like, and the roles it connects to. Click a role to jump to its responsibilities.

PRINCIPLE 1

Journeys Are Living Operational Assets

Journeys evolve and require ongoing stewardship. They are not static documents, they change with our users, services and environment.

In practice: a journey is reviewed monthly and updated whenever the service changes, not left to gather dust after the project ends.
✓
Good governance: every live journey has a review date and a named owner, and updates are logged when the service evolves.
Journey OwnerJourney Manager
PRINCIPLE 2

Evidence Over Opinion

Insights should be grounded in credible evidence and real user understanding, not assumption or the loudest voice in the room.

In practice: every insight carries a source and date, and is only marked Validated once supported by multiple sources or participants.
✓
Good governance: no insight reaches Validated on a single opinion, the status honestly reflects the strength of the evidence behind it.
Journey CreatorJourney Owner
PRINCIPLE 3

Connect to the Enterprise Journey Ecosystem

Reuse and contribute to the broader journey repository. Journeys should be connected, aligned and consistent across the enterprise, not built in isolation.

In practice: before building, you search existing journeys and link or extend what exists rather than starting from scratch.
✓
Good governance: new journeys reference and connect to related ones, and insights are reused across the repository instead of being recreated.
Journey CreatorJourney Manager
PRINCIPLE 4

Maintain Traceability

Every insight, decision and opportunity should have a source, so we maintain trust, transparency and accountability over time.

In practice: the chain is traceable, every insight links to an opportunity, every opportunity to a solution, each with documented evidence.
✓
Good governance: anyone can follow a decision back to the research that informed it, with source, date and owner all recorded.
Journey CreatorJourney Owner
PRINCIPLE 5

Governance Before Publishing

Validate journey quality before publication. We are custodians of quality, journeys should be accurate, secure and fit for purpose.

In practice: a journey passes the completion checklist and a quality review before it is handed over or relied upon for decisions.
✓
Good governance: nothing reaches the live environment unreviewed, and sensitive data is sanitised before any source is attached.
Journey CreatorJourney Manager
PRINCIPLE 6

Prioritise Outcomes, Not Documentation

Journeys exist to improve experiences and services, not to create maps. The map is a means, the outcome is the point.

In practice: opportunities are prioritised against business goals and linked to delivery, not left as a tidy diagram with no action.
✓
Good governance: the journey is judged by the decisions and improvements it drives, not by how polished the map looks.
Journey OwnerJourney Creator
PRINCIPLE 7

Maintain a Single Source of Truth

Avoid duplication and fragmentation. We maintain journeys in shared, trusted and accessible repositories so everyone works from the same picture.

In practice: test content stays in staging, duplicates are merged, and the live environment holds one trusted version of each journey.
✓
Good governance: there is one obvious place to find the current journey, with no competing versions to second-guess.
Journey ManagerJourney Owner
PRINCIPLE 8

In practice: an Owner welcomes new evidence and delegates day-to-day maintenance to a Sub-Journey Owner, rather than guarding the journey.

Good governance: contribution is encouraged and easy, ownership is about care and accountability, not control.

What the Journey Manager is responsible for

The Journey Manager is responsible for keeping the ecosystem trustworthy and usable as it grows. In practice this means:

Standards & consistency

  • Maintaining journey management standards
  • Supporting consistency across TheyDo
  • Reviewing journey structure and layout

Support

  • Supporting Journey Creators and Journey Owners
  • Helping with maintenance and updates
  • Supporting healthy use of the live TheyDo environment

Oversight

  • Running lightweight audits
  • Identifying duplicate journeys
  • Ensuring test journeys are created in the correct staging area
  • Following up when governance issues appear
!
Example, staging vs live. If someone creates a test journey in the live TheyDo space instead of the staging area, they should expect the Journey Manager to contact them and request that it be moved.

Lightweight Governance in Practice

Governance here is light by design, a set of regular, low-friction checks rather than approval gates. In practice the Journey Manager is checking things like:

  • Journey naming conventions are followed
  • Journeys have owners
  • Insights have source information
  • Test content is in the correct space
  • Outdated journeys are flagged for review
  • Journey layouts are consistent
  • Duplicate journeys are identified and resolved

The three roles in the ecosystem today

Creators build and hand over. Owners stay accountable. The Journey Manager keeps the whole ecosystem healthy.

Journey Creator

Build and hand over

  • Build the journey in TheyDo at project end
  • Tag insights, attach evidence
  • Walk stakeholders through the journey
  • Teach them to use the tool, then move on
Journey Owner

Stay accountable

  • Accountable for the journey staying live
  • May delegate day-to-day work to a Sub-Journey Owner
  • Approves changes, signs off direction
  • Decides what gets prioritised
Journey Manager

Govern and support

  • Maintains standards and consistency
  • Runs lightweight audits, resolves duplication
  • Supports Creators and Owners
  • Keeps the live environment healthy
